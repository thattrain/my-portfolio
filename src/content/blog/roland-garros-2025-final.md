## When I Sat Down to Watch

I had no idea what I was about to see. I cleared my afternoon, made coffee, settled in — half expecting a great match, maybe a classic. What I got was five hours that made me question whether anything in sport could ever top it. The 2025 Roland Garros men's final between Carlos Alcaraz and Jannik Sinner is not just the greatest tennis match I've ever watched. According to more than a few people who've seen nearly everything, it's the third greatest men's match of the Open Era — behind only the 2008 Wimbledon final and the 2018 Djokovic-Nadal semifinal. I believe it.

![Sinner and Alcaraz competing at Roland Garros 2025](https://images.tennis.com/image/private/t_16-9_768/f_auto/tenniscom-prd/ijziizdhckkzld4k8xuq.jpg)

## Two Sets Down and Already in Trouble

Sinner took the first set 6-4, clinical and controlled. Then a tiebreak in the second went 7-4 to Sinner again. Two sets to love. I remember thinking: this might already be over. Alcaraz looked out of sorts, a step slow, like the clay was working against him. Sinner, meanwhile, was in one of those zones where everything goes in — deep, flat, relentless. I almost turned it off to go for a walk.

I didn't, and I'm still grateful for that. Because what came next is the part that gets replayed in my head. Alcaraz didn't panic. He didn't go for broke in some desperate way. He just started playing — more aggressive through the middle of the court, higher margin on the forehand, more variation. The third set went 6-4 to Alcaraz, and suddenly it felt like a match again. That shift in momentum wasn't loud. It crept in quietly, and then it was just there.

![Alcaraz match point save at Roland Garros 2025](https://images.tennis.com/image/private/t_16-9_768/f_auto/tenniscom-prd/i3bvwztb2a6xwknsrogq.jpg)

## Three Match Points. Three Times He Refused.

The fourth set is where I lost my composure completely. Sinner had match points. Multiple times, Alcaraz was one shot away from losing. Three match points in total — and he saved all three. I don't know how you do that. I don't know how your legs still work, how your arm doesn't tighten up, how your brain stays clear enough to play the right ball at the right time when everything is on the line. But Alcaraz did it. He forced a fifth set, and the Philippe Chatrier crowd — already on their feet for most of the match — became something else entirely.

Sinner had his own goal-line stand in the fifth. Down but not broken, he clawed back, pushed Alcaraz to another tiebreak. At that point the match had already been going for over four hours. Both players were running on something beyond physical energy — some combination of will and stubbornness and the refusal to be the one who flinches first.

## 7-6 in the Fifth

The final tiebreak went 7-2 to Alcaraz. After everything — two sets down, three match points saved, a fifth-set battle that could have gone either way — he closed it out cleanly. 4-6, 6-7(4), 6-4, 7-6(3), 7-6(2). More than five hours. The longest Roland Garros final in the Open Era.

![Alcaraz winning Roland Garros 2025](https://www.atptour.com/-/media/images/news/2025/06/08/23/01/alcaraz-roland-garros-2025-final-clock.jpg)

## What This Match Means

A friend texted me while I was watching: *"Just when I thought the glory days were over."* He meant it as a joke about his own afternoon plans falling apart — but I kept coming back to that line. Because in a strange way, this match felt like the opposite of that. It felt like the glory days arriving. Alcaraz and Sinner are 21 and 23. This is the beginning of something, not the end. The super-charged pace, the finesse, the athleticism, the mental strength both of them showed — it suggests we're going to be watching these two men trade blows on the biggest stages for the next decade.

I've watched a lot of tennis. I've seen great matches. But I've never quite felt what I felt watching this one — that particular combination of tension and beauty and disbelief that comes when two players are giving absolutely everything they have, and it still isn't enough to separate them until the very last point. That's what sport is supposed to do. This match did it better than almost anything I've ever seen.
